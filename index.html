Antarctic

Antarctic is a polar region located on the very south of our planet. It consists of Antarctica - the coldest, most desolate contintent on the Earth, the islands that suround it, and the waters of the Southern Ocean. It's distant and mysterious beauty has attracted many explorers and scientists, that tried to discover its secrets and set their foot on the places never touched by a human being. The unfavorable, severe conditions that rule this region were a huge obstacle on the road to knowledge about Antarctic. But it didn't discourage them. Will you also fall in love with this place, where white colour shines with a million shades?

Geography & Climate

Everything south of the 60oS lattitude is in the Antarctic. This region covers about 20% of the Southern Hemisphere.

The Southern Ocean that surrounds Antarctica is on of the most dangerous places to sail in the world. Ships encounter there many ice bergs and strong storms. The water is transported quite rapidly because of the Antarcitc Circumpolar Current and the wind is very strong there. So the height of the waves can reach as much as 23m. The waters near the shore are totally covered in ice during the winter, so no ship can get to the continent then.

The continent itself takes up 14 million km2. About 98% of its surface is covered by ice, which is 1.6 km thick on average. Antarctica is the coldest, driest, and windiest continent, and has the highest average elevation of all the continents. The most famous place there is probably the Geographical South Pole. But there's way more to it than this. There are rivers, subglacial lakes, moutains, plateaus, ice caves and even an active volcanoe. The ice walls that guard the entrance to this amazing place are said to be one of the most unforgettable views.

Wildlife

The central part of the continent is filled with ice. But the coast, the islands and the surrounding waters are full of life. We can encounter there many types of algae, bacteria, penguins and other birds, seals, fishes, orcas and whales. There are no polar bears though - they live in the Arctic.

History

Antarctica was the last discovered region on Earth. It was first seen in 1820, but the first landing took place not until 1895. Since then many expeditions were carried out to explore this mysterious place, draw maps and do scientific research. Going there demands a great courage up to this day. Now people can use the help of modern technology - motor sledges, satellite communication, airplanes etc. But back in the early years of polar expeditions, the explorers could use only their own strenght, knowledge and resourcefulness. Building the first stations, enduring the terryffing polar night, going further and further into the heart of the continent was an enormous challenge. Many tried it, and many failed, even losing their lives. But it's commonly said that once you go there, it'll magnetise you for the rest of your life. Its raw charm cannot be forgotten.

Why it's so important

Polar regions are crucial for the stability of the ecosystems on the entire Earth. The ice of Antarctica holds 80% of the world's freshwater reserves. If it melts, the global sea level will rise by about 60 meters! That's enough to sink many cities of our civilized world. Moreover, the enormous white ice deserts reflects the sunshine from its surface and lowers the global temperature tremendously. The scientific reasearch carried out in the Antarctic helps us with weather forecasts and understanding the magnetic field and the geological history of our planet. This continent is one of a kind - a precious white gem of the Earth, shining within the stormiest of oceans.
